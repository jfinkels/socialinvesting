Socially Responsible Investing

Notes on socially responsible investing for the confused young investor.

Suggest changes on GitHub jfinkels/socialinvesting

Introduction

Socially responsible investing is confusing to me. Information is spread out across the Web, and much of it is on old and poorly constructed websites. Here is a collection of information that I have discovered while spending many hours researching investments. Please comment if you think I’ve made a mistake or have something helpful to add.

Investment categories

Socially responsible investments usually include investments in one or more of the following categories:

  • Community development. Usually these are personal or business loans specifically to low- and moderate-income individuals. Some domestic loans in this category meet the requirements of the Community Reinvestment Act (CRA), a United States federal law designed to encourage lending institutions to provide for the credit needs of people from neighborhoods with lower incomes. The organizations Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Ginnie Mae offer CRA-qualified loans. An individual can buy into mutual funds which are a collection of loans such as these.
  • Renewable energy and environmental concerns. Usually, to invest in this category, an individual buys stock in a company which provides products or services for renewable energy. One might also buy into a mutual fund, which is a collection of many stocks which meet some criteria.
  • Local, organic, and/or sustainable farming. To invest in this category as an individual, one usually needs to find a geographically targeted investment fund which provides loans to coops, organic/sustainable farms, etc.
  • Microfinance. These are investments to very poor individuals or groups who might not otherwise be able to receive a traditional loan backed by a bank. Unlike the above categories, microfinance investments are usually not domestic. An individual can make microfinance investments in several ways. One can buy into a social investment fund managed by an institution, with a minimum investment on the order of $1000 to $2500. One can also make loans using websites which pool smaller investments (on the order of $20 to $100) by individuals and provide the sum of those smaller investments to the institutions which make the loans. For even finer grained control, there are websites which allow individuals to pool small investments to loan money directly to individuals.
  • Direct investment in startups. The JOBS Act of 2012 makes crowdfunding for startups possible. Individuals can provide money as startup capital directly to new businesses for a share of the profits. This idea is similar to the idea behind Kickstarter, Indiegogo, or RocketHub, except that projects on these three sites do not return profits to the individual who contributes money.

Some investment funds make investments in several of these categories. Such funds are sometimes called "social investment funds" or "community investment funds".

How to invest

In general, one can make socially responsible investments in these categories by

  • buying into a fund managed by a financial institution which loans to individuals,
  • buying stock in a socially responsible, publicly-traded company, or
  • making smaller investments through microfinance institutions, in "small" companies, or through loans to individuals.

Advantages and disadvantages of investing in a managed collection of stocks, bonds, or loans

Advantages of buying into a fund which makes many loans or investments and which is managed by a team of experts include:

  • a team of experts will do the research on borrowers to determine if they meet the socially responsible criteria of the fund
  • a team of experts will do the research on borrowers to determine if they are likely to repay their loan
  • reduced overhead cost of managing multiple loans compared to single loans

Disadvantages include:

  • smaller risk implies a smaller rate of return, since risk is amortized over multiple investments

Where to send my money

Publicly traded stocks and bonds and mutual funds consisting of such securities can be bought and sold using an online broker. The online broker buys the stock, bond, or mutual fund on your behalf and provides supplementary information about its current performance, profile, and history.

To buy into a social investment fund, one usually must send an application and a check to the institution itself. Such investments cannot be tracked using an online broker, as far as I can tell, unless explicitly stated by the fund. For example, Calvert Foundation’s Community Investment Note provides CUSIP numbers (which is just a finance term for a universally unique identification number) so that one can purchase the notes through a broker (for example, an online broker), but RSF’s Social Investment Fund does not.

To make a microfinance loan or investment through a web service, payments can be made directly on the website of the service.

Return on investment

Some investments, like social investment funds or microfinance investments, have a fixed interest rate, usually between 0% and 4%. Others, like stocks or mutual funds, have variable returns which depend on the economic success of the product or service. In general, socially responsible investments enjoy smaller profits when compared with socially irresponsible investments. Also keep in mind that mutual funds or other collections of securities have smaller risk and therefore smaller reward (but smaller losses when a stock tanks).

Screening for certain types of investments

Mutual funds may explicitly state the types of companies in which they invest and those in which they do not invest. Active investment in a certain type of company is called "positive screening", and active non-investment in a certain type of company is called "negative screening". The United States Social Investment Forum (US SIF) has done the research on many popular mutual funds designed for socially responsible investment to determine their screening practices. Where to find investments

In this section, I provide some links to websites I have found helpful, plus some suggestions for investment opportunities which, according to my amateur research, seem legitimate.

Mutual funds

The following socially responsible mutual funds seem reasonably successful:

  • New Alternatives Fund (NALFX)
  • CRA Qualified Investment Fund (CRATX)

Microfinance

The following websites allow individuals to fund microfinance loans with small amounts of money:

  • Kiva. These are charitable loans which backfill loans made by microfinance institutions, and do not provide income due to interest. Note that money paid does not go directly to the person whose face is above the "Lend now" button.
  • Microplace. These are investments which fund microfinance institutions to make specific types of loans.
  • Zidisha. These are loans made by a group of lenders (you and other individuals) directly to borrowing individuals without any intermediary microfinance institution (besides the website itself).

Startups

The following websites allow individuals to fund startups with small amounts of money:

  • Wefunder. This site will (after the SEC concludes its rulemaking period for the JOBS Act, which should conclude approximately in January, 2013) allow individuals to invest some "small" amount of money into startups (on the order of $1000).
  • EarlyShares. Same as above.
  • SeedInvest. Same as above.
  • CrowdFunder. Same as above.
  • Gambitious. Same as above, but for startups which produce video or computer games.
